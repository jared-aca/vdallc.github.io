TDIU: How Veterans Can Receive 100% VA Pay Without a 100% Rating

If your service-connected conditions prevent you from holding down substantially gainful employment, you may qualify for TDIU — which pays at the 100% rate regardless of your combined rating percentage.

What Is TDIU?

Total Disability based on Individual Unemployability (TDIU) is a VA benefit that allows veterans to be compensated at the 100% disability rate even when their combined disability rating is below 100%. The key isn’t the number on the rating sheet — it’s whether your service-connected conditions, taken together, make it impossible to maintain substantially gainful employment.

TDIU exists because the VA’s schedular rating system sometimes fails to fully capture how a combination of conditions — none individually rated at 100% — can effectively end a veteran’s working life. When that gap exists, TDIU is the mechanism designed to bridge it.

The Two Paths to TDIU

There are two ways to qualify for TDIU. The first is called schedular TDIU, and it requires meeting specific numerical thresholds. The second is extraschedular TDIU, which applies when those thresholds aren’t met but the circumstances still warrant the 100% rate.

Schedular TDIU requires one of the following:

  • A single service-connected condition rated at 60% or higher, or
  • Multiple service-connected conditions with a combined rating of 70% or higher, where at least one condition is individually rated at 40% or higher

Extraschedular TDIU does not require meeting those numerical thresholds. It applies when a veteran’s particular situation — the specific nature, combination, and functional impact of their service-connected conditions — creates unemployability that the rating percentages alone don’t reflect. Extraschedular cases go through a different adjudicative path and require more detailed documentation of the functional impairment.

Important: TDIU is evaluated on service-connected conditions only. Non-service-connected conditions — even severe ones — do not count toward the unemployability determination.

What “Substantially Gainful Employment” Actually Means

The VA defines substantially gainful employment as work that produces income above the federal poverty threshold. This definition matters in several important ways. First, it means veterans who work part-time or in sheltered employment (work accommodated specifically around their disabilities) may still qualify for TDIU. Second, it means that holding any job, by itself, does not automatically disqualify a veteran from TDIU.

What the VA is asking is whether your service-connected conditions prevent you from competing in the open labor market for employment that pays above poverty-level wages. Veterans who can only hold low-wage, heavily accommodated, or intermittent positions due to their service-connected conditions may still meet the unemployability standard.

The Application Process

Applying for TDIU requires filing VA Form 21-8940 (Veteran’s Application for Increased Compensation Based on Unemployability). The form asks for a detailed employment history, educational background, and a description of how your service-connected conditions affect your ability to work. You will also need to authorize the VA to contact former employers using VA Form 21-4142.

Medical evidence is central to a TDIU claim. The record should document not just the diagnoses but their functional impact — how the conditions interfere with the physical or cognitive demands of sustained employment. A well-developed TDIU claim pairs the rating evidence with vocational and functional analysis that connects the dots between the medical record and the employment barrier.

Common Pitfalls

Several patterns lead to TDIU denials that could have been avoided:

  • Claiming unemployability without documenting the functional limitations that cause it — a diagnosis alone is not sufficient
  • Failing to account for all service-connected conditions in the application, especially mental health conditions that affect cognitive endurance
  • Working in a marginal or accommodated position and assuming that disqualifies you — it typically does not
  • Leaving the effective date question unaddressed, which can result in a later effective date and loss of retroactive benefits

If TDIU was denied because the VA found you capable of substantially gainful employment but the record doesn’t support that finding, an appeal is likely the right next step. The strength of the functional evidence in the record often determines the outcome at the Board level.
